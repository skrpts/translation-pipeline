## Core Tone Principles

Every customer interaction must reflect these four principles:

1. **Empathetic** — Acknowledge the customer's situation before moving to solutions. They need to feel heard, not processed.
2. **Clear** — Use plain language. Avoid jargon, internal terminology, and ambiguous phrasing. If the customer needs to re-read your message, it was not clear enough.
3. **Solution-focused** — Lead with what you *can* do, not what you cannot. Every response should move the conversation towards resolution.
4. **Never blame the customer** — Even when user error is the cause, frame it neutrally. The goal is to fix the problem, not assign fault.

## Tone Shifts by Situation

### Routine Enquiry
- **Tone:** Friendly, efficient, lightly conversational
- **Approach:** Answer directly. Offer helpful extras where relevant ("You might also find it useful to..."). Keep it brief — respect their time.

### Complaint
- **Tone:** Empathetic, ownership-driven
- **Approach:** Acknowledge the impact on the customer first. Take ownership ("We should have caught that" rather than "There was an error"). Apologise sincerely — once, clearly, without over-apologising. Then move to resolution.

### Technical Issue
- **Tone:** Patient, step-by-step, encouraging
- **Approach:** Never assume technical knowledge. Number your steps. Confirm understanding before moving on. Celebrate progress ("That's sorted the first part — now let's tackle the next bit").

### Escalation
- **Tone:** Calm, transparent, reassuring
- **Approach:** Explain what is happening and why ("I'm bringing in a specialist who can resolve this faster"). Give a realistic timeline. Never promise what you cannot guarantee.

## Language: Use vs Avoid

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| "I understand how frustrating that must be" | "You should have done X instead" |
| "Let me look into this for you" | "That's not our department" |
| "Here's what I can do" | "There's nothing I can do" |
| "Thank you for flagging this" | "You're the first person to report this" |
| "I appreciate your patience" | "You'll just have to wait" |
| "Let me make sure I've understood correctly" | "You're not making sense" |
| "That's a fair point" | "Actually, if you read the documentation..." |
| "We got this wrong — I'm sorry" | "Unfortunately, our policy states..." (as a deflection) |
| "I'll follow up by [specific time]" | "Someone will get back to you" |
| "Would you prefer X or Y?" | "You need to do X" |

## De-escalation Phrases and Patterns

When a customer is upset, use this sequence:

1. **Acknowledge** — "I can see why that's frustrating" / "You're right to be concerned about this"
2. **Align** — "If I were in your position, I'd feel the same way"
3. **Assure** — "Let's get this sorted for you right now"
4. **Act** — Move immediately to a concrete next step

### Useful De-escalation Phrases

- "I hear you — let's fix this together."
- "That shouldn't have happened, and I'm sorry it did."
- "You've been more than patient. Let me prioritise this."
- "I want to make sure we get this right for you."
- "Let me take personal responsibility for seeing this through."

### Patterns to Avoid During Escalation

- Repeating scripted phrases — it signals you are not listening
- Saying "calm down" or "relax" — it invalidates their feelings
- Deflecting to policy without acknowledging the impact first
- Offering discounts or credits before understanding the real issue

## Channel-Specific Tone Adjustments

### Email
- **Thorough and structured.** Use headings, numbered steps, and clear paragraphs.
- Summarise the issue back to the customer at the top of your reply.
- Close with a clear next step and timeline.
- Tone can be slightly more formal than chat, but never stiff.

### Live Chat
- **Concise and responsive.** Short paragraphs — ideally two to three sentences per message.
- Use the customer's first name naturally (once, not repeatedly).
- Signal when you are looking something up ("Bear with me a moment — just checking your account now").
- Avoid long blocks of text. Break information across multiple messages if needed.

### Phone
- **Warm and conversational.** Let your personality come through.
- Mirror the customer's pace — if they are speaking slowly and carefully, match that.
- Use verbal confirmations ("Got it", "Makes sense", "I see") to show you are listening.
- Summarise agreed actions before ending the call.
