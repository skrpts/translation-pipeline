## Email Formatting Standards

### Subject Lines
- Start with a clear topic, not a greeting ("Q3 Budget Review — Approval Needed" not "Hi team")
- Use prefixes for clarity: **[Action Required]**, **[FYI]**, **[Decision Needed]**, **[Update]**
- Keep under 60 characters where possible
- Never leave the subject line blank when replying — update it if the topic has shifted

### Greetings and Sign-offs
- **Internal:** "Hi [Name]," or "Morning team," — keep it natural
- **External (known contact):** "Dear [Name]," for first contact, then "Hi [Name]," once rapport is established
- **External (formal):** "Dear [Title] [Surname],"
- **Sign-off internal:** "Thanks," / "Cheers," / "Best,"
- **Sign-off external:** "Kind regards," / "Best regards," / "Many thanks,"

### Paragraph Length
- Maximum four sentences per paragraph
- One idea per paragraph
- Use line breaks between sections — dense blocks of text go unread
- For emails longer than three paragraphs, add a TL;DR at the top

## Tone Rules

### Internal Emails
- Conversational but professional
- Direct — get to the point in the first sentence
- It is fine to use contractions ("we'll", "I've", "that's")
- Assume good intent; avoid passive-aggressive phrasing

### External Emails
- Slightly more formal, especially on first contact
- Mirror the recipient's tone after the initial exchange
- Avoid internal shorthand, project codenames, or assumed context
- Always provide enough background for the recipient to understand without chasing

## Action Item Formatting

Action items must be unmistakable. Use this format:

- **[Owner]** — Description of the action — **by [deadline]**

Examples:
- **Sarah** — Send revised proposal to client — **by Friday 14 March**
- **Dev team** — Deploy hotfix to staging — **by end of day**
- **All** — Review the attached document and add comments — **by Wednesday**

Rules:
- Bold the owner's name so it stands out on a quick scan
- Bold the deadline
- One action per bullet point — never bundle multiple actions together
- If there is no deadline, state "no fixed deadline" rather than leaving it blank

## Meeting Summary Structure

Every meeting summary email should follow this format:

**Subject:** [Meeting Name] — Summary — [Date]

**Body:**

1. **Attendees:** List of who was present (and note key absences if relevant)
2. **Key Decisions:** Bullet list of what was agreed — be specific ("Approved Q3 budget of £120k" not "Discussed budget")
3. **Action Items:** Use the action item format above
4. **Open Items:** Anything unresolved that needs further discussion
5. **Next Steps:** When the next meeting is, or what happens next

Keep the entire summary to one page or less. If the meeting ran long, summarise — do not transcribe.

## Abbreviations and Acronyms Policy

- **First use:** Spell out in full, followed by the abbreviation in brackets — "Key Performance Indicator (KPI)"
- **After first use:** Abbreviation only is fine
- **Common exceptions** that do not need spelling out: UK, US, CEO, HR, IT, PDF, URL
- **Internal acronyms** (project names, team codes) must always be spelled out in external communications
- When in doubt, spell it out — clarity always wins over brevity

## CC/BCC Etiquette

### CC
- Only CC people who genuinely need visibility but are not expected to act
- If you are CCing someone's manager, have a good reason — it can feel like escalation
- When replying to a CC'd thread, consider whether all CCs still need to be included
- Never use CC as a passive way to "keep someone in the loop" on a conflict

### BCC
- Use BCC for large distribution lists to protect email addresses
- Never BCC someone to secretly include them in a conversation — it damages trust
- Acceptable use: mass announcements, external newsletters, introductions (BCC the introducer after the intro is made)

### Reply vs Reply All
- **Reply All** only when your response is relevant to everyone on the thread
- When in doubt, reply to the sender only and ask if they want you to share with the group
- If a thread has more than five Reply Alls, consider moving the discussion to a call or chat
